Impact of Blockchain on Society

By Muhammad Saud, Huzaifa Awan, M. Abbas

Sindh Madrassatul Islam University, Karachi

Abstract

Blockchain is a system that keeps track of transactions, particularly those involving cryptocurrencies, among computers connected by a peer-to-peer network. The three waves of digitalization—the digitalization of facts represented by the internet, the digitalization of communities and tools for community building represented by social media, and the digitalization of value represented by blockchain—are discussed along with the history of blockchain technology. The paper explores how society, or some aspects of society, have changed throughout the course of time since the introduction of blockchain. After a brief explanation of the terminology, a map of the possibilities that blockchain technology holds for society is drawn.

Introduction

When Satoshi Nakamoto originally launched Bitcoin as a peer-to-peer electronic cash system in 2008, he first articulated the blockchain concept (Nakamoto, 2008). Without having a significant impact on society, the idea of blockchain and similar technologies were only explored in specialised computer science and cryptography circles in the years after the publishing of Bitcoin. Economics or commerce. In some ways, this is similar to the early days of the Internet, which have their roots in the ARPANET, a TCP/IP-based early packet routing network created at the end of the 1960s and utilised mainly for communication and sharing of resources in learning and military organisations (Oppliger, 1998). Around the year 2015, not only did the price of Bitcoin start to skyrocket, but also a number of books were released that primarily catered to practitioners and defined the possible financial benefit of the innovation (Swan, 2015; Tapscott and Tapscott, 2016). As a result, the general public's awareness of blockchain significantly increased.

Related Work

This subsection provides a thorough analysis of related Blockchain research. This review's objective is to analyse and summarise the results and contributions of numerous studies in the subject, as reflected by the cited academic journals. The chosen papers offer a well-rounded picture of the present status of Blockchain research by covering a variety of viewpoints and approaches. This review analyses these works in order to highlight the major approaches, trends, and issues in the field of Blockchain research as well as possible future directions.

The study uses a framework to look into the blockchain framework in the first article, which focuses on blockchain technology. The contributions of the writers shed light on the distinctive features and capabilities of blockchain technology and offer insightful information about its architecture. There is also discussion of the potential gaps in knowledge or future research directions.

Methodology

The initial idea put forth by Satoshi Nakamoto in the Bitcoin whitepaper is the beginning point for the writers' review of the body of information already available on blockchain technology. They look at how the concept of blockchain was initially only popular in specialised computer science and cryptography circles but rapidly gained popularity once the price of Bitcoin increased and books written for practitioners were published. The use of blockchain technology in Dubai's CommerCity free-trade zone for e-commerce and supply chain strategies in large corporations (IBM, Maersk, Carrefour, Walmart) are just two instances of real-world examples of blockchain adoption in diverse industries examined by the writers.

They look into how blockchain might improve various industries' security, efficiency, and transparency with the goal of doing away with middlemen in peer-to-peer transactions. The potential for blockchain technology to transform and strengthen computer applications in the public and private sectors is explored in this study. It examines how blockchains can offer an archive of agreements with a cryptographic audit trail, allowing for in-the-moment resource and agreement monitoring. The authors also address privacy issues and outline substantial barriers to blockchain's widespread acceptance as a decentralised cryptocurrency. The authors examine how people perceive cryptocurrencies and how they have been covered in the media, covering topics like scepticism and fascination among economists and financial experts, worries about their connection to illegal activity on the dark web, and discussions about whether they can be used to solve economic problems. The authors raise academic interest in examining the viability and issues related to researching the influence of blockchain technology on society based on the examination of the existing literature and case studies. They also suggest prospective research gaps.

Conclusion

This essay has examined the idea of blockchain technology and its implications for society. The three waves of digitalization—the internet, social media, and ultimately blockchain—have been examined, demonstrating how blockchain has become a transformational force in the digital sphere. From its original debut with Bitcoin through its adoption by a number of industries, including banking, finance, supply chain, and e-commerce, the history of blockchain technology has been reviewed in this study.

The potential for blockchain technology to fundamentally alter data possession and management through the rise of self-sovereign identities (SSIs) is one of this study's primary findings. Individuals are given greater ownership and control over their personal data thanks to blockchain-based SSIs, which also offer a safe, decentralised method of sharing that data with others. This may lessen unauthorised online surveillance and censorship while having important implications for privacy and data protection.

The study also emphasised the many advantages of blockchain technology, such as accountability, egalitarianism, decentralisation, and transparency. These fundamental operational principles may be able to resolve the problems brought on by an over-reliance on centralised institutions in charge of controlling access to online information. By utilising blockchain technology, it is possible to lessen abuses of human rights and advance an inclusive and open digital society. The report does, however, also recognise the real-world constraints and problems related to blockchain adoption. Despite its enormous promise, there are currently substantial barriers preventing its complete adoption, especially in terms of scaling, energy use, and regulatory frameworks. For blockchain technology to reach its full potential, future research and development initiatives should concentrate on overcoming these issues.

In summary, this essay has given a thorough introduction of blockchain technology and its social ramifications. It has drawn attention to the revolutionary potential of blockchain in a number of industries and stressed the need for more investigation and study to fully grasp its viability and handle any potential issues. We can realise the full promise of blockchain technology and build a more open, safe, and egalitarian digital future by encouraging scholarly curiosity and interdisciplinary cooperation.
